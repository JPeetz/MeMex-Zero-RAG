# The Memex: Vannevar Bush's 1945 Vision

Source: "As We May Think" - The Atlantic, July 1945
Author: Vannevar Bush

## The Original Vision

In 1945, Vannevar Bush (director of the Office of Scientific Research and Development) proposed a device called the "memex" — a mechanized private file and library.

## Key Concept

"A memex is a device in which an individual stores all his books, records, and communications, and which is mechanized so that it may be consulted with exceeding speed and flexibility. It is an enlarged intimate supplement to his memory."

## Associative Trails

Bush's key insight was that human memory works by association, not indexing. The memex would allow users to create "trails" linking related information:

"The process of tying two items together is the important thing... thereafter, at any time, when one of these items is in view, the other can be instantly recalled."

## Why It Matters Today

The web became something different — public, chaotic, organized by search engines rather than personal association. Personal wikis and knowledge bases are closer to Bush's vision:

- Private, curated collection
- User-defined associations (links)
- The connections as valuable as the documents

## The Missing Piece

Bush couldn't solve the maintenance problem. Who builds and maintains these trails? In 1945, it would be the user doing tedious mechanical work.

In 2026, the answer is clear: LLMs. They don't get bored, don't forget to update cross-references, and can process documents faster than any human.

"The Memex is finally buildable." — Andrej Karpathy, 2026
